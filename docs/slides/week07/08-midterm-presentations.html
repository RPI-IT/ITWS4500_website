Section 2: Architecture Deep Dive

5-7 minutes (most important section)

Present 2-3 Architectural Decision Records:

# ADR-001: [Decision Title]

## Context
What situation required a decision?

## Decision
What did you choose?

## Consequences
- Positive: What improved?
- Negative: What trade-offs did you accept?
- Risks: What could go wrong?

## Alternatives Considered
What else did you evaluate and why was it rejected?

Example ADR Presentation

ADR-001: Use Zustand for State Management

Context: Our app has complex cart state shared across many components. We needed a solution that avoided prop drilling and re-render issues.

Decision: We chose Zustand over Context API and Redux.

Why not Context? Context causes re-renders of all consumers on any state change. With our cart updating frequently, this caused performance issues.

Why not Redux? Too much boilerplate for our team size and timeline. Redux Toolkit helped, but Zustand was simpler.

Trade-off: Less ecosystem (middleware, devtools) than Redux, but acceptable for our scope.

Section 3: Live Demo

3-5 minutes

Demonstrate:

  • Core user flow (most important feature)
  • Data persistence (show data in MongoDB)
  • Architecture in action (how layers interact)

Demo Tips

  • Have a backup plan (screenshots, video)
  • Pre-load data to avoid empty states
  • Test your demo environment before presenting
  • Keep it focused—don’t show everything

Section 4: Lessons Learned

2-3 minutes

Reflect honestly on:

  • What worked well?
  • What would you do differently?
  • What challenges remain?
  • What are your next steps?

This section shows maturity—we’re not expecting perfection!

Presentation Quality 15% Clear communication, time management

What Makes an “A” Presentation?

Architecture Understanding: - Correctly applies course concepts - Shows clear separation of concerns - Demonstrates understanding of trade-offs

Decision Quality: - ADRs have clear context and rationale - Alternatives were genuinely considered - Trade-offs are acknowledged

Implementation: - Demo works smoothly - Code follows discussed patterns - Evidence of testing/quality

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

❌ Too much code walkthrough - Show architecture, not every file

❌ No trade-offs discussed - Every decision has downsides

❌ Demo-heavy, architecture-light - Features matter less than design

❌ Rushed Q&A - Save time for questions

❌ No backup for demo - Technology fails; be prepared

❌ Vague ADRs - “We chose React because it’s popular” isn’t sufficient

Effective Presentations

Know Your Audience

You’re presenting to:

  • Instructors who know the concepts (don’t over-explain basics)
  • Classmates who don’t know your specific project
  • Technical audience who appreciates depth

Balance: - Enough context for understanding - Enough depth to show mastery - Not so much detail that you lose people

Visual Communication

Good slides:

  • Diagrams over bullet points
  • Code snippets (short, focused)
  • Screenshots of key UI
  • Architecture visualizations

Avoid:

  • Walls of text
  • Tiny font sizes
  • Full source files
  • Reading slides verbatim

Handling Q&A

Prepare for common questions:

  • “Why didn’t you use X instead?”
  • “How does Y scale?”
  • “What would you do differently?”
  • “How do you handle Z edge case?”

Good responses:

  • Acknowledge the question
  • Give a direct answer
  • Explain reasoning
  • Admit uncertainty if appropriate (“We haven’t tested that yet, but…”)
